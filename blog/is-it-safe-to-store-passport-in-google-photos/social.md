## LinkedIn Post

Most people store their passport scan in Google Photos without thinking twice.

You need a copy. You take a photo. It backs up automatically. Done.

Here's the problem: Google Photos was built for holiday snaps and family portraits. Not for documents that contain your full legal name, date of birth, passport number, and biometric photo — all in a single image.

The risk isn't really Google doing something sinister. It's more mundane.

A credential-stuffing attack hits your Google account. The password was reused from a breach three years ago on a different site. You've forgotten about it. The attacker hasn't. Now everything in your Google Photos is accessible — including your passport scan.

Your name, date of birth, nationality, and passport number together are enough to attempt identity fraud or run a convincing phishing attack using your real details.

The safer options, in order:

1. **On-device encrypted app (no cloud upload)** — no server ever touches your passport data. This is the strongest option.
2. **Zero-knowledge password manager** (1Password, Bitwarden) — end-to-end encrypted before anything hits their servers.
3. **iCloud with Advanced Data Protection enabled** — extends end-to-end encryption to photos, but it's off by default.
4. **Google Photos with strong 2FA + unique password** — acceptable, not ideal.
5. **Camera roll with no encryption** — the weakest option of all.

If you're already using Google Photos for this: turn on two-factor authentication, audit which third-party apps have access to your account, and use a password you've never used anywhere else.
