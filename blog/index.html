Why I Built
Travel Document Vault

MH

Mustafa Hafeez

Jan 3, 2026

I was standing at immigration when the officer looked at my daughter's passport, paused, then looked up at me.

"You know this expires in seven months, right?" he said. "Just making sure you're aware - some countries won't let you in with less than six months validity."

I thanked him. We were fine - barely. But the whole trip, I kept thinking: if he hadn't mentioned it, I wouldn't have noticed. Seven months felt like plenty of time. I'd checked the passport before booking. But I'd completely forgotten about the six-month rule that most countries enforce.

That close call - realizing I'd almost ruined a family trip without even knowing it - stayed with me. I'd set up calendar reminders for everyone's passport expiry dates. I thought that was enough. But I wasn't checking the actual dates - I was just trusting the reminders. And when someone's passport got renewed, I'd have to update the reminder, update the scan in Google Drive, and hope I didn't miss anything.

That moment - that pit in your stomach when you realize you might have just ruined a family trip - is why I built this app.

The invisible work

If you're the person in your family who manages documents, you know this feeling.

You're not just storing files. You're carrying an invisible checklist in your head. Which passports expire when. Who needs what for school. Which countries have the six-month rule. Whether the travel insurance actually got renewed or if you just meant to do it.

I calculated once: checking one passport properly - opening the file, reading the dates, doing the math on validity, googling the destination's entry rules - takes about 10 minutes. For a family of four going abroad, that's 40 minutes just for passports. Add travel insurance (another 5 minutes to find the policy and check it's current). Add driver's licences if you're renting a car (5 more minutes to verify they're not expired).

An hour. Gone. Every single trip.

And that's if everything's organized. If you're digging through email for that insurance PDF you think you downloaded, or trying to remember which cloud folder has the passport scans, double that.

Nobody sees this work. There's no checklist anyone hands you. You just... carry it.

I didn't want to build a travel app. I wanted to stop carrying this in my head.

What I tried first

I tried everything before building this.

Cloud storage? Works for files, but doesn't tell you when they expire. I still had to open every document manually and do the mental math.

Calendar reminders? I tried that. Set a notification six months before each passport expired. But then the document scans lived in one place, the reminders in another, and when I actually needed to check something at 11pm before booking a flight, I was jumping between three different apps trying to piece it together.

Password managers? Some people swear by them for storing document scans. But they're built for passwords, not for tracking expiry dates. And they definitely don't remind you when your kid's passport is about to make you miss a trip.

Here's what finally clicked for me: I didn't need another place to save PDFs. I needed something that would actually remind me six months before the expiry date, not on the expiry date. Something where I could see "okay, we're traveling in August, does everyone's passport last until February?" without doing mental math or googling visa requirements.

And it had to work offline. Because I'd be at the airport, or in a hotel lobby with terrible Wi-Fi, trying to show someone a document copy, and that's when cloud apps decide to stop loading.

So I built exactly what I needed

Travel Document Vault does one thing: it puts everything you need in one place, so you never have to think about it again until the app reminds you.

Scan a document once. The app reads the expiry date (you double-check it's right). Reminders schedule themselves automatically: six months early, then three months, six weeks, one month, two weeks, down to the day before it expires.

One profile per family member. Every document in one spot. When you need to check if you're good to travel, you open the app and you know. Immediately. Not "probably" or "I think so" - you know.

And when a hotel needs your passport copy, or an embassy needs your details, or the car rental place asks for your licence, you share it in one tap. No digging. No Wi-Fi required. Everything together, right there.

That's the payoff: you get your brain space back. The invisible mental load - the constant low-level worry about whether you're forgetting something - it's gone. The app remembers, so you don't have to.

It's not just passports

Once I started using it, I realized how many other documents quietly expire in the background.

Work permits if you're living abroad. Visas and residency cards that need annual renewal. Driver's licences that expire every few years. Health insurance that renews annually (and you need to prove coverage). Travel insurance policies. The kids' national IDs.

They all have expiry dates. They all matter at the exact wrong moment. And nobody reminds you until it's too late - or you're already at the embassy realizing your visa expired last month.

The app handles all of them. Anything with an expiry date - passports, visas, work permits, insurance, licences, whatever your family needs - one place, tracked, handled.

When things go wrong abroad

Over 300,000 passports are lost or stolen every year in the US alone. If it happens to you abroad, you're not starting from zero.

Document numbers. Expiry dates. Clear scans. Ready when you need to file a report or contact an embassy. It doesn't replace your original documents, but it gives you what you need to move forward - without Wi-Fi, without panic.

Designed for privacy

Early on, I made a core decision about how this app should work: your documents belong to you, and they should stay only with you.

Travel Document Vault is self-contained:

  • No accounts to create
  • No server upload
  • Encrypted on your device

When you scan a document, text recognition runs on your device. Everything stays on your phone. The app does not upload them to any server.

Some people prefer apps that sync everywhere. But for passports and IDs, I wanted a simpler model: your phone is the vault. You can export your copies whenever you need to. They are yours, after all.

Why pay once instead of subscribe

A travel document vault is something you set up once and forget about. It sits there quietly tracking expiry dates until you need it. That's the whole point.

A subscription doesn't make sense for that. If I stop paying, I'd need to export everything, find another app, migrate my documents, set up reminders again. And if I forget to renew? I lose access right when I might need it most - standing at airport check-in realizing my kid's passport expires in 4 months and I can't get to the scan I stored.

When you buy this app, it's yours. Your documents stay on your device. The reminders keep working. No renewals. No exports. No vendor lock-in. It just works, quietly, in the background, until you need it.

Why this exists

I built Travel Document Vault because I wanted to stop being the anxious parent at the gate.

I wanted to be the one pointing out the planes to my kids, not the one switching between apps to find the right document. I wanted to know - actually know, not hope - that everything was sorted before we left the house.

If you're the person in your family who double-checks everything, who keeps the folder, who worries quietly so everyone else doesn't have to - I built this for you.

One less thing to worry about.

Built for personal use first. No accounts. No server upload.
