Why I Built Travel
Document Vault

MH

Mustafa Hafeez

Jan 3, 2026

I was booking flights when it hit me.

Not the flights - those were easy. It was the step after. I needed to make sure I had everyone's documents sorted: passports for the whole family, my driver's licence for the rental car, travel insurance details, the kids' vaccination records.

So I did what I always do: started checking. Opened Photos - scrolled past hundreds of screenshots looking for the passport I took a photo of last year. Was it my son's or my daughter's? Opened Google Drive - saw three insurance files, couldn't remember which one I'd actually bought. Searched my email - found my licence as an attachment from two phones ago. The kids' passports? I'd recently renewed them but never saved copies anywhere.

I'd been doing this before every trip for years. And every time, I'd end up with the same uneasy feeling: I think I have everything. Probably.

"Probably" is not a word you want in your head when you're about to fly with your family.

The invisible work

If you're the person in your family who keeps track of the documents, you know what I mean.

You're the one who remembers that most countries won't let you in if your passport expires within six months. You're the one who knows the kids' passports expire every five years, not ten. You're the one who checks, double-checks, and quietly worries so everyone else can get excited about the trip.

Nobody sees this work. There's no checklist anyone hands you. You just... carry it. Alongside everything else — the school forms, the medical appointments, the permission slips, the meal plans.

I didn't want to build a travel app. I wanted to take one thing off that list.

What I tried first

I tried everything before building this.

A folder in Photos called "Documents." It worked until it was buried under 3,000 holiday snaps. Cloud storage with offline mode — fine for storage, but then I needed a calendar app for expiry reminders, and I still had to remember which folder had which kid's documents. Notes app with photos pasted in — sitting right next to my grocery list, unencrypted.

I even tried a couple of travel apps. One wanted me to create an account and upload my passport to their servers. Another charged $49 a year — for what was basically a file manager with a calendar.

Every solution was either too scattered, too expensive, too invasive, or too generic. None of them understood that a passport is not just a file. It's a document that expires, that belongs to a specific person in your family, and that you need at the worst possible moment — often without Wi-Fi.

So I built exactly what I needed

Travel Document Vault does a small number of things, and it does them properly.

You scan your family's documents once. The app reads the expiry dates automatically - no typing, no manual entry. It sets reminders that start six months early and cascade down: three months, six weeks, one month, two weeks, right down to the day. One profile per family member. Everything organized, everything tracked. And when a hotel, embassy, or travel agent needs a document, you share it in one tap - no digging through five different apps.

And it all stays on your phone.

No accounts. No cloud. No servers. Your documents are encrypted on your device with the same standard banks use. I can't see your data. I don't want to.

It's not just passports

Once I started using it for passports, I realized how many other documents I was quietly tracking. Driver's licences. Health insurance cards. The kids' national IDs. Travel insurance policies. Vaccination records.

They all expire. They all matter at the worst possible moment. And nobody reminds you until it's too late.

So the app handles all of them. Anything with an expiry date your family depends on - one place, one app, handled for you.

When things go wrong abroad

Over 300,000 passports are lost or stolen every year in the US alone. If it happens to you abroad, you're not starting from zero.

Document numbers. Expiry dates. Clear scans. Ready when you need to file a report or contact an embassy. It doesn't replace your original documents, but it gives you what you need to move forward - without Wi-Fi, without panic.

Designed for privacy

Early on, I made a core decision about how this app should work: your documents belong to you, and they should stay only with you.

Travel Document Vault is self-contained:

  • No accounts to create
  • No server upload
  • Encrypted on your device

When you scan a document, text recognition runs on your device. Everything stays on your phone. The app does not upload them to any server.

Some people prefer apps that sync everywhere. But for passports and IDs, I wanted a simpler model: your phone is the vault. You can export your copies whenever you need to. They are yours, after all.

Why pay once instead of subscribe

A travel document vault is something you set up once and forget about. It sits there quietly tracking expiry dates until you need it. That's the whole point.

A subscription doesn't make sense for that. If I stop paying, I'd need to export everything, find another app, migrate my documents, set up reminders again. And if I forget to renew? I lose access right when I might need it most - standing at airport check-in realizing my kid's passport expires in 4 months and I can't get to the scan I stored.

When you buy this app, it's yours. Your documents stay on your device. The reminders keep working. No renewals. No exports. No vendor lock-in. It just works, quietly, in the background, until you need it.

The free version gives you one profile, five documents, OCR, reminders, and a PIN lock. Pro unlocks unlimited profiles and documents with a single payment. That's it.

Why this exists

I built Travel Document Vault because I wanted to stop being the anxious parent at the gate.

I wanted to be the one pointing out the planes to my kids, not the one frantically scrolling through Photos to find a passport scan. I wanted to know - actually know, not hope - that everything was sorted before we left the house.

If you're the person in your family who double-checks everything, who keeps the folder, who worries quietly so everyone else doesn't have to - I built this for you.

One less thing to worry about.
