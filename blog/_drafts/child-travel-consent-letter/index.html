Child Travel Consent Letter: When You Need One and What It Must Say

April 15, 2026 7 min read

Travelling with children across borders involves more than just a passport. In many countries, authorities require a child travel consent letter (also called a parental consent letter, letter of authorization, or letter of permission) when a child travels with one parent, with neither parent, or with a guardian. Yet requirements vary dramatically by destination, and getting it wrong can mean your family is turned away at the border.

Key Takeaways

  • Requirements vary significantly by country; a letter that works for one destination may not be accepted elsewhere
  • Most countries require the letter to be notarised or certified by a solicitor or notary public
  • The letter must include child's full name, date of birth, passport number, travel dates, destinations, and contact details for absent parent(s)
  • Common rejections result from missing information, lack of notarisation, outdated signatures, or insufficient contact details
  • You can store encrypted digital copies alongside travel documents, but carry a printed copy as backup

Who Actually Needs a Child Travel Consent Letter?

The short answer is: it depends entirely on your destination. Requirements vary hugely by destination country, border control policies, and your family's specific circumstances. There's no universal rule, which is why many families get caught off guard.

Situations where a letter is commonly required:

  • A child travels with only one parent. Immigration officers want to verify the absent parent hasn't objected to the trip. Some countries require this; others don't.
  • A child travels with neither parent. The letter authorises a grandparent, aunt, uncle, or other guardian to travel with the child. Most countries require explicit consent from both parents in this scenario.
  • A child travels with one parent whose surname differs from the child's. This can raise flags, especially for younger children, but is not required everywhere.
  • A child is adopted or in foster care. Legal guardianship documents and consent may be required alongside the letter. Rules vary by destination and vary again if you're a UK resident versus a citizen abroad.

Critical caveat: Some countries have no formal requirement at all. The Netherlands, for example, doesn't mandate a consent letter even if a child travels with one parent. Others—like Canada and South Africa—effectively require one for nearly all international travel scenarios. Still others have requirements only for children under certain ages, or only if the travelling parent is not the primary custodian. There is no global standard.

Always verify requirements directly with the immigration authority for your specific destination country before travelling. Use IATA Travel Centre to check entry requirements, or contact your destination country's embassy or high commission. If you're unsure, having the letter is better than the alternative: being stopped at check-in or the border.

What Must Be Included in the Letter

A child travel consent letter is not a casual note. It's a formal document, often notarised, that must contain specific information. Here's what needs to be in every letter:

Essential Information Checklist

  • Child's full name and date of birth (as it appears on the passport)
  • Child's passport number
  • Proposed travel dates (departure and expected return)
  • Destinations (all countries the child will visit)
  • Name and relationship of the accompanying adult
  • Contact information for the absent parent(s) (full name, phone number, email)
  • Explicit consent statement ("I/We hereby grant permission for...") signed by the absent parent(s)
  • Signature(s) of the absent parent(s)
  • Date of signature
  • Notarisation or certification (if required by destination)

The letter should be written in a formal, professional tone. Vague language ("my child can travel whenever") won't work. Border officials need to see that the absent parent explicitly consents to this specific trip, on these specific dates, to these specific places. A typical letter might read: "I, [Full Name], hereby grant permission for my child [Child's Full Name], passport number [number], to travel to [destination(s)] departing on [date] and returning on [date], accompanied by [Travelling Adult's Name and Relationship]. I consent to this travel and authorise the travelling companion to make decisions concerning my child's welfare during this trip."

Some border officers will phone the contact number provided to verify the absent parent's consent. Make sure that number is correct, is answered by the person named, and that they can confirm on the phone that they gave permission. If you cannot guarantee someone will answer during border hours, provide an alternative contact and note it in the letter.

Notarisation and Official Certification

In most cases, the letter must be notarised—meaning a notary public or solicitor signs and stamps it, verifying that the absent parent signed it in their presence. In some jurisdictions, a solicitor's certified signature is acceptable instead of notarisation.

Countries that typically require notarisation:

  • Canada – Notarised or certified; child protection laws are strict
  • South Africa – Certified by a notary public or attorney; requirements are very strict
  • Australia – Notarised or certified by a solicitor; often required even for domestic travel
  • United Kingdom – Generally recommended and often required by airlines
  • United States – Not federally mandated but recommended; some states recognise notarisation

What this means in practice

You're planning to take your daughter to Canada with your ex-partner's notarised consent. When you apply for the consent letter, your country's requirements may not match Canada's. A UK solicitor's certified signature might not satisfy a Canadian border officer who expects a formal notarisation. You need to research the destination country's specific rules, not just your home country's standards. Costs and timelines differ vastly: getting a letter notarised in Australia might take three weeks and cost AU$150, whereas a UK solicitor letter might be faster and cheaper. Start the process at least four weeks before travel.

Notarisation typically costs between $20–$100 (or equivalent in your currency) per signature, depending on location. Plan for this cost when preparing your documents, and allow 1–2 weeks to schedule a notary appointment, especially during school holidays. Always confirm your destination's requirements first—it determines whether you need notarisation at all.

Common Mistakes That Get Letters Rejected

Even well-intentioned families make errors that lead to rejection at the border. Here are the most frequent:

  1. Missing or unclear contact information. If the phone number doesn't work or the person who answers can't verify consent, the letter loses credibility.
  2. No specific travel dates. Vague dates like "summer 2026" are too broad. Use exact departure and return dates.
  3. Unsigned by the absent parent. A letter signed only by the travelling parent is worthless. Both parents (or legal guardians) must sign if both have legal custody.
  4. Missing notarisation when required. Some countries will turn your family away if the letter isn't officially certified.
  5. Outdated signature. If the letter was signed 2+ years ago, border officers may question whether it's still valid consent.
  6. Wrong passport number or child name. Even small discrepancies raise red flags.
  7. Incomplete destination list. If the letter says "Europe" but doesn't name specific countries, it may be rejected.
  8. No explicit permission statement. The letter must clearly state that the absent parent consents to the trip.

Review your letter carefully before presenting it at the border. Have a second person check for typos, missing signatures, or incomplete information.

Storing and Managing Your Travel Consent Letters

Once you've obtained notarised consent letters for all your destinations, the next challenge is keeping them safe and accessible during travel.

The practical approach is to maintain three versions:

  1. Original notarised copy. Keep this at home in a secure location (safe, safety deposit box, or dedicated file).
  2. Printed travelling copy. Carry this in your passport or travel document folder.
  3. Encrypted digital backup. Store a scanned copy securely so you can access it if the printed version is lost or damaged.

Digital storage is increasingly accepted, but practices vary. Some border officers still insist on the original document. That's why carrying both is the safest approach.

Final Checklist Before Travel

In the days before departure, run through this checklist to ensure your consent letters are in order:

  • Verify requirements with each destination country's immigration authority (use IATA Travel Centre or contact the embassy directly)
  • Confirm all names (including middle names), passport numbers, and dates match across all documents
  • Check that all required signatures are present, dated, and match the name of the signing parent
  • If notarised or certified, verify the notary's seal, stamp, signature, and date are clear and current (typically no older than the date shown in the table above)
  • Confirm the contact number for the absent parent is correct and they are available and willing to answer verification calls from border officials
  • Carry a printed copy in your travel documents folder, preferably in a protective sleeve
  • Store a high-resolution scanned copy securely (encrypted cloud backup or device storage) as a digital backup
  • If your child's passport is renewed before travel, obtain a fresh consent letter with the new passport number
  • Bring the letter in its original form if it was notarised or certified—don't laminate it, as lamination can invalidate certification

A well-prepared consent letter is your family's safeguard against travel disruptions. The time spent now—gathering signatures, arranging notarisation, and storing copies—pays dividends in smooth border crossings and peace of mind.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a child travel consent letter

It depends on your destination. Requirements vary significantly by country and circumstance. You typically need one when a child travels with one parent, with neither parent, or with a guardian. Some countries require them for all international travel scenarios; others have no requirement at all. Always verify with the immigration authority for your specific destination. Use IATA Travel Centre to check your destination country's rules.

Does a child travel consent letter need to be notarised

In most countries, yes. Canada, South Africa, Australia, and the UK typically require notarisation or certification. However, some destinations accept a witnessed signature from a solicitor or notary public. Requirements vary by destination and by your home country. Check with your destination country's immigration authority to confirm their specific requirements before you obtain or sign the letter.

What happens if child travel consent letter is rejected at border

Border officers may refuse entry or prevent the child from travelling. In serious cases, this can trigger child protection inquiries if authorities suspect a kidnapping or dispute. Common rejection reasons include missing information, lack of notarisation, outdated signatures (usually older than 1–2 years), missing passport number, or contact details for absent parents that don't answer or don't confirm consent. Verify all requirements with your destination before travel, and have a second person review your letter for errors.

How long is child travel consent letter valid for

Most authorities consider a letter valid for 12 months from the date signed or notarised, though some may accept longer validity (up to 2–3 years). However, many countries want a fresh letter if the child's passport is renewed before travel. The safest approach is to carry a recently signed version (within the last 6–12 months) and verify specific validity rules with your destination country before departure. Always check the passport expiry date too—many countries require at least 6 months validity beyond your travel dates.

Can you store child travel consent letter digitally

Many countries now accept digital copies on mobile devices or printed from digital files, but requirements vary. Some border authorities still insist on seeing the original notarised document. Best practice is to carry both a printed copy in your travel documents folder and a secure digital backup accessible from your phone.
