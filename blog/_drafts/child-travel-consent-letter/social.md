## Twitter/X Thread

**Tweet 1:**
Your child's passport is valid. Your ticket is booked. The border officer still won't let them through. Why? No consent letter—or the wrong one. Here's what every parent should know before travelling with kids across borders.

**Tweet 2:**
First: Do you even need one? It depends. Canada? Generally yes. Netherlands? No. Australia? Sometimes. South Africa? Nearly always. There's no global rule. This is why checking with your destination country matters. Start here: iatatravelcentre.com

**Tweet 3:**
Second: What must be in it. Full names. Passport numbers. Specific travel dates and destinations. Contact details for the absent parent. An explicit permission statement. And here's the critical bit: the signature of the absent parent, not just yours.

**Tweet 4:**
Third: Notarisation rules vary wildly. Canada and South Africa want it formal and certified. Australia needs a solicitor's stamp. The UK leaves it to airlines. The US doesn't federally require it. Your home country's rules don't matter—only your destination's do.

**Tweet 5:**
Final tip: A letter that works in one country won't work in another. Store three copies: original (home safe), printed (in your travel folder), and encrypted digital (on your phone). And when you're at the airport, not the border, is when you confirm that everything's correct.

## Instagram Carousel Script

**Slide 1 (Hook – Dark blue background, gold text)**
"Your consent letter doesn't work here."
The border officer hands it back.

A child travel consent letter isn't one-size-fits-all. It's destination-specific. Get it wrong, and your family could be stopped before they board.

**Slide 2 (Light text on dark blue)**
Do you even need one?

It depends on where you're going. Some countries require a letter when a child travels with one parent. Others don't. Some have no requirement at all.

Always check your destination country first. Don't guess.

**Slide 3 (Light text on dark blue)**
What it must contain:

Child's full name & date of birth
Child's passport number
Specific travel dates
All destination countries
Name of travelling companion
Contact details for absent parent(s)
Explicit permission statement
Signature of absent parent
Date

Missing any of these? It gets rejected.

**Slide 4 (Light text on dark blue)**
Notarisation rules change by country.

Canada: Always notarised
South Africa: Certified by attorney
Australia: Solicitor certified
UK: Recommended (airlines require it)
US: Varies by state
EU: Check each country

Don't assume your home country's rules apply.

**Slide 5 (Light text on dark blue)**
Common mistakes that get letters rejected:

Wrong passport number
Unsigned by absent parent
Vague travel dates ("summer")
No contact details
Missing notarisation
Signature older than 2 years

One error = travel disrupted.

**Slide 6 (Light text on dark blue)**
How to protect yourself:

Verify with your destination immigration authority
Carry 3 copies: original (home), printed (with you), digital (encrypted on phone)
Confirm absent parent's contact details work
Check 4 weeks before travel
Never laminate notarised documents
