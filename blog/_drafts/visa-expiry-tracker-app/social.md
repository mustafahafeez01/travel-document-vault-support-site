# Social Media Assets — Visa Expiry Tracker App

## LinkedIn Post

I watched a traveller miss the most costly mistake possible, and it was entirely preventable.

She'd checked her visa expiry: "Valid until June 30". Booked her trip for June 20-28. Felt secure.

What she didn't realise: the visa validity date and the maximum stay duration are two different things. Her visa was valid until June 30, but she could only stay for 30 days from entry. She entered June 20. She had to leave by July 20.

But she'd already planned to stay until June 28 — before realising the rule — so she was fine this time.

Thousands aren't. They overstay by days. Sometimes hours. The penalties are severe: re-entry bans lasting years, fines, deportation records.

The problem: no one teaches travellers this distinction. It's not on the visa sticker. It's not in the government information. You have to know to look for it.

I've written a full guide to what visa tracking actually requires — the dates that matter, the ones people confuse, and how to avoid this trap.

## Twitter/X Thread

**Tweet 1:**
Your visa says "valid until 31 Dec". You think you can stay until 31 Dec. You're probably wrong. Visa validity dates and permitted stay dates are different things. And that gap is where most overstays happen.

**Tweet 2:**
A visa has three dates that matter: (1) when the visa sticker expires / (2) the last day you can enter / (3) the last day you can stay once you've entered. Most travellers only watch one of them. Two of these are often completely different.

**Tweet 3:**
The Schengen 90/180 rule is worse: it's a rolling window, not a calendar reset. 90 days in any 180-day rolling window. If you visited Schengen 6 months ago, those days still count against today. Most travel apps don't calculate this at all.

**Tweet 4:**
What does overstaying cost. US: 180+ days = 3-year re-entry ban. 1+ year = 10-year ban. Schengen: banned across all 27 countries. Australia: 3-year exclusion. It follows you. It's in the database they check at every border.

**Tweet 5:**
If you travel more than once a year, the solution is simple: track your visa dates separately from the sticker validity. Separate reminders for entry validity, maximum stay, and entry count. Record the day you enter. Calculate your departure deadline immediately.

## Instagram Carousel Script

**Slide 1 (Hook)**
Your visa doesn't expire when the sticker does. You entered June 20. The sticker says "valid until August". You have 30 days. You must leave by July 20. Not August.

Confused yet. Most travellers are.

**Slide 2**
The problem: visas have three separate dates — and most people only watch one. (1) Entry validity / (2) Maximum stay duration / (3) Entry count (single vs multiple). They're all different. They all matter.

**Slide 3**
Schengen visitors: your 90 days resets in a rolling 180-day window, not on January 1st. Days you spent in Europe 6 months ago still count. A basic calendar won't track this.

**Slide 4**
What overstaying actually costs you:
US: 3-10 year re-entry ban
Schengen: banned in 27 countries
Australia: 3-year exclusion
Thailand: daily fines + deportation

It stays in your immigration file. Forever.

**Slide 5**
To avoid this: record your entry date immediately. Calculate your latest permitted exit date the same day. Set a reminder 2 weeks before. Track your maximum stay separately from the sticker date.

**Slide 6**
If you travel multiple times a year, use an app that tracks both dates separately. Offline access matters — you might need to check at a border. Privacy matters too — visa data is sensitive.

## Brand Notes

**Visual concept (dark blue + gold):**
- Dark blue background (#003A87 or #0A192F)
- Gold accent for key dates and numbers (#FFB000)
- Clean, minimal layout — no excitement, high clarity
- Icon concept: calendar split in half (entry validity vs. maximum stay) in gold
- Font: sans-serif, readable at small sizes

**Tone:** Calm, practical, slightly urgent but not alarmist. The stakes are real; the information is precise.
