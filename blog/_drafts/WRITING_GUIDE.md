# Blog Writing Guide — Travel Document Vault

This is the house style guide for every blog article on traveldocumentvault.com/blog/. Read this before writing or editing any article.

---

## Gold Standard — The Bar Every Article Must Clear

Every article must pass all of these before it's publishable. No exceptions.

**Engagement**
- The opening paragraph must create a genuine "that's happened to me" or "I didn't know that" reaction in the first 3 sentences. If it doesn't, rewrite it.
- Every H2 section must contain at least one concrete, specific fact — a number, a country, a deadline, a real consequence. Vague paragraphs get cut.
- If a reader can skim just the takeaways box + section headings and get the full picture, the structure is working. If not, fix the headings.

**Readability**
- Maximum sentence length: 25 words. Break anything longer into two sentences.
- Maximum paragraph length: 4 lines on mobile. Break anything longer.
- No paragraph should start with the same word as the one before it.
- Read every paragraph aloud. If you stumble, shorten it.

**Length discipline**
- Target 1,200–1,600 words of prose. Hitting 1,800+ requires a genuine reason (multiple countries, highly complex topic).
- If you're over 1,600 words, cut the weakest section entirely — don't trim. Weak sections add length without value.
- Tables and FAQ answers do not count toward the word count target.

**Zero AI slop** (see full list below — this is enforced)
- No filler openers: "In today's world", "Whether you're a seasoned traveller or a first-timer", "Planning your next adventure"
- No padding conclusions: "In summary", "To recap", "As we've seen"
- No vague intensifiers: "very important", "extremely useful", "highly recommended"
- If a sentence can be deleted without losing any information, delete it.

**Quality check before saving**
1. Does the hook make you want to keep reading?
2. Does every section answer a real question someone would type into Google?
3. Are all factual claims linked to an official source or qualified with "typically/generally/check with your authority"?
4. Is the CTA natural — not forced?
5. Would a stressed parent at 11pm find this genuinely useful?

If any answer is no, fix it before moving on.

## Who We Are Writing For

The reader is typically a parent planning an international family trip, or an individual who travels a few times a year and wants to be organised. They are not travel experts. They are not reading for entertainment — they have a specific question and want a clear, practical answer.

They trust content that is:
- Specific and verifiable (real rules, real countries, real consequences)
- Honest about uncertainty ("requirements vary", "check with your airline")
- Written like a knowledgeable friend, not a government leaflet or a marketing team

## Voice and Tone

**The voice is: calm, practical, trustworthy.**

Think of a senior colleague who travels regularly and knows the rules. They don't hype things up. They don't hedge everything into uselessness. They tell you what they know, flag where things vary, and point you to official sources for the rest.

**Good reference point:** The tone of a Which? or Consumer Reports article. Or a well-written GOV.UK guidance page.

**Not:** Travel blog listicle. Not a product landing page. Not an AI-generated summary.

### Specific tone rules

- Write in second person ("you", "your passport") for most articles
- Use first person ("we", "I built this") only for the founder story
- Address the reader as an adult who can handle nuance — don't over-explain
- Be direct. Say "you cannot board a flight without a valid passport." Not "it may be the case that boarding could potentially be affected."
- Short sentences work. Long compound sentences with multiple clauses are harder to parse, particularly for readers who may be reading on a phone while stressed about their trip.

## Non-Negotiable Rules

These are enforced without exception across every article:

**1. No exclamation marks. Anywhere.**
Not in headings. Not in the CTA. Not in the disclaimer. Not even in the App Store link alt text.

**2. No absolute claims.**
Never write "always", "never fails", "100% secure", "guaranteed", "every country", "all airlines". Use: "typically", "generally", "in most cases", "many countries", "requirements vary".

**3. The article must be useful without the app.**
A reader who never downloads the app should still find the article genuinely helpful. The app is mentioned naturally once or twice, never as the point of the article.

**4. App pricing: "$9.99 one-time".**
Never say "cheap", "affordable", "low-cost". If price is mentioned, it's always "$9.99 one-time".

**5. Spelling: British English.**
- travellers (not travelers)
- organise (not organize)
- recognised (not recognized)
- cancelled (not canceled)
- licence (noun) / license (verb)
- practise (verb) / practice (noun)

**6. No "passport photos" when you mean document scans.**
The app stores scans/copies of documents. A "passport photo" is the biometric photo printed in the passport or submitted for a new one. These are different things. Never conflate them.

**7. Legal disclaimer on every article.**
Every article ends with a disclaimer section (glass-panel styled). The wording should be tailored to the topic — immigration articles are different from storage articles. General template:

> "This article is for general information only. [Topic-specific caveat]. Always verify current requirements with official government sources before travelling."

## No Ambiguity Rule (Zero Tolerance)

If a rule, requirement, or process varies by circumstance (country of residence, nationality, age, expat status, etc.) — **you must call that out explicitly**. Never write a statement that is true for some readers but false for others without flagging the distinction.

**Examples of unacceptable ambiguity:**
- "UK passport renewal doesn't require posting your old passport" — TRUE for UK residents, FALSE for expats abroad. Must say both.
- "You can travel while your passport renewal is pending" — TRUE if you still have your passport, FALSE if you've submitted it. Must say both.
- "The 6-month rule doesn't apply in Europe" — TRUE for most of Schengen, but some countries require 3 months beyond departure. Must qualify.

**The test:** Read every claim and ask "Is this true for ALL readers, or only some?" If only some, add the caveat.

**Format:**
> "If you are applying from within the UK: [rule]. If you are a UK citizen living abroad: [different rule]."

Always link to the official source where the reader can confirm which applies to them.

## Official Sources Rule (Zero Tolerance)

Every country-specific factual claim must link to an official government or authority source. No exceptions. Never use travel blogs, forums, or aggregator sites as sources.

## Pre-Publish Checklist (Every New Article)

Before saving any new or upgraded article, verify all of the following:

**Structure**
- [ ] Takeaways box present with 5 specific, standalone takeaways
- [ ] 5–6 H2 sections, each covering a distinct aspect
- [ ] At least one comparison table or structured list
- [ ] Mid-article CTA nudge (gold border box, natural mention of app)
- [ ] "What this means in practice" callout box (blue border) in the most complex section
- [ ] FAQ section — 5 questions phrased as exact Google searches, self-contained answers
- [ ] Related articles aside — 2 cards, most directly relevant posts
- [ ] Disclaimer — tailored to the topic
- [ ] Bottom CTA — scroll-fade-in, App Store badge

**Spacing & layout**
- [ ] `<main>` tag uses `class="max-w-6xl mx-auto px-4 sm:px-6 lg:px-8 pt-24 sm:pt-32"` — no bottom padding on main (footer provides its own)
- [ ] Footer has `pt-20 pb-24 md:pb-10` padding (sufficient top gap above footer links)
- [ ] CTA section has `pt-24 pb-24` padding (breathing room above and below)

- [ ] Disclaimer section uses `mb-8 mt-12` (spaced from article and CTA)

**Related articles**
- [ ] Both linked articles are actually published (slug exists in `blog/[slug]/`)
- [ ] Related article `href` uses `../[slug]/` — one level up from `blog/[slug]/` (NOT `../../` which resolves to the site root)
- [ ] Related articles genuinely relate to the article topic — not just the two most recent
- [ ] Date shown in related card matches the actual published date
- [ ] Tag pill matches the tag defined for that article in README.md

**Sources & accuracy**
- [ ] Every country-specific claim links to an official source
- [ ] Any rule that varies by circumstance explicitly flags the variation
- [ ] No travel blog, forum, or aggregator links used as sources

## Evidence Standards

**Every factual claim must be one of:**

1. **Common knowledge** that any travel professional would confirm (e.g. "You need a valid passport to board an international flight") — no citation needed
2. **Sourced to an official body** — link directly to the government/authority page (IATA Travel Centre, GOV.UK, travel.state.gov, passports.gov.au, canada.ca, USCIS, etc.)
3. **Qualified with uncertainty** — if you cannot verify it, say "requirements vary — check with your destination country's immigration authority"

**Never:**
- Invent statistics ("millions of travellers are caught out each year") — remove or source it
- State country-specific rules without an official link or clear qualification that these vary
- Copy-paste facts from other travel blogs — trace them to the original source

## Structure Every Article Must Have

**1. H1 — contains primary keyword**
Usually the article title. Must be factual, not clickbait. No question marks in H1 unless the article literally answers that question.

**2. Opening paragraph — hook with substance**
Start with a real scenario or a specific fact that establishes why this matters. Not "in today's world of travel" or "are you planning your next adventure?". Something concrete: a rule, a consequence, a situation the reader recognises.

**3. Key Takeaways box (near the top)**
5 bullet points. Each one a standalone, specific takeaway — not a teaser for what's below. A reader who only reads the takeaways should still understand the main points.

**4. 5–6 H2 sections**
Each section covers one distinct aspect of the topic. H2s should be informative, not clever. "Which Countries Enforce the 6-Month Rule" is better than "The Surprising Truth About Passport Validity".

**5. At least one table or structured list**
For scannability and featured snippet targeting. Country comparison tables, requirement checklists, step-by-step numbered lists.

**6. Mid-article CTA nudge**
A small, gold-bordered box (not a banner, not a screaming CTA) that mentions the app naturally in context. One sentence about what the app does, one link. Appears roughly two-thirds of the way through the article, after the main educational content.

**7. FAQ section (bottom, 5 Q&As)**
Targets "People Also Ask" on Google. Questions should be phrased exactly as someone would type them. Answers should be complete and self-contained — a reader landing directly on a FAQ answer should not need to read the rest of the article to understand it.

**8. Related articles aside**
2 post cards linking to other published articles. Pick the two most directly relevant.

**9. Disclaimer**
Tailored to the article topic. See the rule above.

**10. Bottom CTA section**
The App Store download prompt with the badge image. Brief value proposition (1–2 sentences). Scroll-fade-in animation class.

## What "AI Slop" Looks Like — Phrases to Remove

If you see any of the following, rewrite the sentence from scratch:

- "In conclusion" / "In summary" / "To summarise"
- "It's worth noting that" / "It is important to note that"
- "Dive into" / "Delve into"
- "Tapestry" / "Multifaceted" / "Landscape" (used metaphorically)
- "Game-changer" / "Groundbreaking" / "Revolutionary"
- "Seamlessly" / "Effortlessly"
- "Empower" / "Supercharge" / "Unlock"
- "Robust" (unless describing a literal physical object)
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Comprehensive guide" (in the text — it's fine in a title)
- "Look no further"
- "Invaluable" / "Meticulous" / "Meticulously"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "The bottom line is"
- Any sentence that starts with "Ultimately,"

**The test:** Read a paragraph aloud. If it sounds like no actual person would say it in conversation, rewrite it.

## SEO Requirements

**Primary keyword:**
- In the H1
- In the first 100 words of body text
- In one H2 (exact or close match)
- In the meta description (150–160 characters)

**Meta description format:**
- Contains the primary keyword
- States a clear benefit or answers the question
- 150–160 characters exactly (use a character counter)
- No clickbait, no ALL CAPS, no exclamation marks

**Internal links:**
- Every article links to `/blog/` (anchor: "travel document tips" or "our blog")
- Every article links to one or two related articles on the site
- The App Store link appears once, in the mid-article CTA or the bottom CTA — never in the body prose
- External links to official sources open in a new tab (`target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"`)

---

## Word Count

Target: **1,200–1,800 words** of body text.

Under 1,000 words: too thin for competitive keywords.
Over 2,000 words: needs a strong justification (complex topic, multiple countries covered).

Count only prose — not headings, not the FAQ, not the takeaways box. The FAQ, takeaways, and tables are additional.

## Adding a New Article to the Publish Pipeline

When a new article is written and placed in `blog/_drafts/[slug]/`:

1. Add the publish date, slug, title, tag, and status to the **schedule table** at the top of `_drafts/README.md`
2. Add the **post card HTML** to the "Post card summaries" section of `README.md`
3. Add the **post card case block** to `_drafts/publish.sh` (in the `get_card()` function)
4. Set status to `📝 Not yet written` initially, then `✍️ Draft ready` when the HTML file exists

## When You're Ready to Publish

Run:
```bash
cd ~/Github/support-site
./blog/_drafts/publish.sh [slug]
```

Then follow the steps printed by the script. Pushing to `main` deploys automatically via GitHub Actions (~2 minutes).
