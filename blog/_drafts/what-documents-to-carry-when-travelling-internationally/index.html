What Documents to Carry When Travelling Internationally: The Complete List

Travel Document Vault

March 11, 2026 · 9 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Your passport must typically be valid for at least 6 months beyond your planned return date for most international destinations.
  • Visa requirements depend on your nationality and destination — always verify before you travel, not the week before.
  • Travel insurance documentation belongs in your bag; your policy number and emergency line should be accessible even without internet.
  • Children need their own passports — and potentially a notarised consent letter if travelling with only one parent or with neither parent.
  • Carrying secure offline digital copies of every document is the single best preparation for losing any of them abroad.

Knowing what documents to carry when travelling internationally is one of those things every frequent traveller thinks they have figured out — until they arrive at an airport check-in and discover something is missing, expired, or has the wrong name. This guide covers the full document list, the ones most commonly forgotten, and some practical notes for families travelling with children.

The Non-Negotiables: Documents You Cannot Travel Without

1. A valid passport

Your passport must be valid — but the threshold is higher than most people expect. For the majority of international destinations, airlines and border control require your passport to be valid for at least 6 months beyond your planned departure date from that country, not just your return flight date. This is the 6-month passport rule, and it catches more travellers off guard than almost any other document requirement.

Check your passport validity before booking, not before flying. Renewal times vary significantly by country — in the US, routine renewal currently takes 6–8 weeks; in the UK, up to 10 weeks. Starting the renewal process 6 months before your travel date gives you a comfortable buffer.

2. Visa or entry authorisation (if required)

Whether you need a visa depends on two things: your passport nationality and your destination. Many countries offer visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to citizens of certain countries. Others require a visa obtained in advance. Some countries have recently introduced electronic travel authorisations (ETAs or ETAs-equivalent) for nationals who previously entered visa-free — Australia's ETA, Canada's eTA, and the UK's ETA for EU visitors are examples.

The definitive resource for visa requirements is the IATA Travel Centre, used by airlines worldwide. Your destination country's official government immigration website is also authoritative. Checking well in advance matters: some visas have processing times of several weeks.

Travel Insurance: The Document Most People Forget to Access

Most travellers buy travel insurance. Far fewer can immediately produce their policy number, their insurer's 24-hour emergency number, or confirmation of what their policy actually covers. The document matters most in exactly the scenario where you're least likely to be thinking clearly: a hospital abroad, a stolen bag, a missed connection.

What to have accessible:

  • Policy number
  • Insurer's 24-hour emergency phone number (separate from their general number)
  • A summary of your coverage limits — particularly medical evacuation, which can cost tens of thousands without insurance
  • Pre-existing conditions exclusions, if applicable

This is a document worth storing digitally and in your phone contacts as well as in paper form. If your bag is stolen, you may need it without your physical documents.

What Families with Children Need to Add

Children have their own passport requirements that go beyond the basics. Each child needs their own passport for international travel. Many countries also scrutinise travel by children closely to guard against parental abduction — particularly when a child is travelling with only one parent.

Child travel consent letter: If a child is travelling internationally with only one parent, many border officers will ask for written, notarised consent from the absent parent. If travelling with neither parent — with grandparents, for example — both parents' consent is typically required. Requirements vary significantly by destination, so check the entry requirements for your specific destination with the official immigration authority.

A consent letter should typically include the child's full name and date of birth, passport details, travel dates and destinations, and contact details for the absent parent(s). Some destinations have specific templates — the Canadian government, for example, provides a sample format.

What this means in practice

You're boarding a flight to Canada with your 8-year-old child and your partner is not travelling. Without a notarised consent letter from your absent partner, you'll be denied boarding. If your child is travelling with their grandparents instead of you, both parents must sign the consent letter — the presence of one parent's permission alone will not be sufficient. Always verify the exact requirements for your destination well before your departure date.

The Case for Offline Digital Copies

Carrying certified physical copies of documents alongside originals is standard advice. But there is a practical gap in that advice: if the bag with all your copies gets stolen simultaneously with your originals, you are left with nothing.

Offline digital copies — stored encrypted on your phone — provide a backup stored separately from your physical documents. When your embassy issues an emergency travel document, they need your passport number, date of issue, place of issue, and expiry date. A secure digital copy provides these details instantly, without internet access. Frequently Asked Questions

What documents do I need to travel internationally

At minimum: a valid passport with sufficient remaining validity (typically 6 months beyond your travel dates for most destinations), and any required visa for your destination. You should also carry travel insurance documentation, your flight and accommodation bookings, and secure digital copies of all documents stored offline. Depending on destination and circumstances, vaccination records, an international driving permit, or a child travel consent letter may also be required. Check specific requirements with your destination's immigration authority before you travel.

Do I need physical copies of travel documents

Original physical documents are required at borders and for immigration. Certified physical copies are useful as a backup. Secure offline digital copies are a resilient additional layer — they survive separately from your physical documents and can help your embassy issue an emergency travel document far more quickly if the originals are lost or stolen. A digital copy allows you to provide your passport number, issue date, and place of issue without internet access.

What documents do children need for international travel

Children require their own passport — they cannot travel on a parent's passport. If travelling with only one parent, many countries require a notarised child travel consent letter signed by the absent parent. If travelling with neither parent, both parents' consent is typically required. Requirements vary by destination, so verify with the official immigration authority for your specific destination before travelling.

Is travel insurance documentation required when travelling abroad

Your policy number and insurer's 24-hour emergency number should be accessible at all times. Storing a digital copy offline means you can access it without internet connection. Your insurer's emergency line should be saved in your phone contacts separately, in case you lose access to your physical documents. This is critical if you need medical treatment abroad or have an emergency while travelling.

What happens if I forget my passport when travelling

Forgetting your passport means you generally cannot board an international flight — airlines and border control require original documents. If you discover something is missing after departure, the next step depends on what's missing: some visa types can be obtained on arrival at certain destinations, while a missing passport requires contacting your embassy for an emergency travel document. Digital copies with your passport number and issue details speed up this process significantly.
