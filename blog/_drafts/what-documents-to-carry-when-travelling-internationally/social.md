# Social Media Content — What Documents to Carry When Travelling Internationally

## LinkedIn Post

You're at check-in. The agent scans your passport, then pauses. "Your travel insurance document—where is it?" You check your carry-on. You have it. You're fine.

But your mate in line behind you doesn't. His copy is in his checked luggage. The agent won't accept it. He's not boarding. Not today.

The rule is harder than most realise: the documents you actually need when you board are not the ones in your suitcase.

Here's the breakdown:

**Always on your person (not checked):**
— Passport and visa
— Travel insurance card or policy number (separate, written down)
— Flight booking confirmation
— Any required vaccination certificate

**In your carry-on (accessible during the trip):**
— Accommodation booking
— Digital copies of everything (encrypted, offline)
— International driving permit (if hiring a car)
— Medication documentation
— For families: child travel consent letters

**In checked luggage only:**
— Extra passport photos (for renewals)
— Certified backup copies

The best single move you can make: offline digital copies on your phone. Encrypted. Not synced to cloud. Not relying on WiFi. If you lose the physical documents, your embassy can process an emergency travel document in hours instead of days.

Core documents that must be on your person:
— Valid passport (6 months minimum validity for most destinations)
— Visa or entry authorisation (if required)
— Travel insurance documentation and policy number
— Flight booking confirmation
— Any vaccination certificate required by your destination

Verify everything well in advance with iatatravelcentre.com.

**Tweet 3**
Family vs solo vs business — the list changes:
Solo travellers don't need child consent letters. Families require notarised consent if a child travels with one parent absent. Business travellers usually have corporate insurance but still need personal policy details. Each trip type has different document priorities.

**Tweet 4**
