## LinkedIn Post

Your passport application came back after three weeks with two words stamped on it: "Photo rejected."

The reason? Shadows under your eyes. You'd taken the photo in your bedroom with what you thought was perfect window light. The application office doesn't care about perfect. They care about the specifications.

Passport photo requirements are brutally specific. Get the background, lighting, or dimensions wrong—even slightly—and your application gets returned. Processing delays. Missed travel plans. Extra costs.

Here's what actually matters when you take a passport photo at home: a plain white background with no shadows, natural window light positioned to avoid harsh shadows, the right size for your country (these vary), and a neutral expression. No smiling.

The difference between accepted and rejected often comes down to one detail you didn't know to check.

We've written a guide covering everything officials look for when they review your photo—what rejects photos, how to set up your lighting, the exact size requirements for the US, UK, Australia, and Canada, and common mistakes you can avoid before you hit submit.

## Twitter/X Thread

**Tweet 1**
Your passport photo got rejected. The reason: "shadows on the face." You took it in your bedroom with what you thought was good light. The passport office disagrees. There are rules. Specific ones. They matter more than how good your photo looks to you.

**Tweet 2**
Passport photo requirements vary by country, but the core rules are the same everywhere. Plain white background. No patterns, no textures, no shadows. Neutral expression. Eyes open. Eyes looking straight at the camera. Face taking up roughly 50% of the frame. Size exact to the millimetre.

**Tweet 3**
Why does size matter this much. The biometric database used by immigration and border control depends on precise face measurements. If your photo is too small or too large, the system can't match your face to future documents. So they reject it and ask you to resubmit.

**Tweet 4**
Lighting is where most home photos fail. Position yourself perpendicular to a window so light hits your face evenly. Avoid direct sunlight at midday—it creates harsh shadows. Never use camera flash. If you're getting shadows, reposition or wait for cloudier weather.

**Tweet 5**
Size requirements vary by country: US 2x2 inches, UK and Australia 35x45mm, Canada 50x70mm. Each has specific rules for face height and head room. Download a passport photo app for your country, crop your smartphone photo to spec, and check the dimensions twice before you print or upload.

**Slide 2**
First rule: plain white background. No patterns, colours, shadows, or textures. Just white. (This is non-negotiable — they'll reject you.)

**Slide 3**
Lighting matters more than you think. Position yourself perpendicular to your window. Light should hit both sides of your face evenly. Never use camera flash.

**Slide 4**
Size specifications vary by country and must be exact. US: 2x2 inches. UK and Australia: 35x45mm. Canada: 50x70mm. Check your official passport authority before you crop or print.

**Slide 5**
Expression rules: neutral face, eyes open, looking straight at the camera. No smiling. No unusual expressions. No glasses unless medically necessary and approved by your country's authority.

**Slide 6**
Common rejection reasons: shadows on face, wrong size, smiling, blurry image, coloured background, improper cropping, wrong lighting. Avoid one of these and your application gets returned.
