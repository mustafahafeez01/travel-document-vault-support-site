How to Take a Passport Photo at Home That Actually Gets Accepted

April 29, 2026 7 min read

Your passport application has been sitting in the office for three weeks. The reason: your photo was rejected for "shadows on the face". You took it in your bedroom with natural light and thought it looked fine. The photograph requirements for passport applications are strict, specific, and they vary by country. Get the background, lighting, or size wrong by even a few millimetres, and your application gets returned. This guide walks you through exactly how to take a passport photo at home that actually passes on the first attempt.

Key Takeaways

  • Use a plain white or off-white background, neutral expression, and eyes open and looking straight ahead
  • Size requirements vary by country: US (2x2 inches/51x51mm), UK and Australia (35x45mm), Canada (50x70mm)
  • Natural window light works best; avoid harsh shadows and avoid using camera flash
  • Most countries now prohibit glasses unless medically necessary; check current requirements
  • Common rejection reasons include shadows, wrong size, smiling, blurry photos, and improper backgrounds

Universal Requirements Across Countries

Although specific rules vary by passport authority, most countries share core requirements for passport photos. Understanding these fundamentals will put you on solid ground regardless of which country issues your passport.

Background and Composition

The background must be plain white or off-white. This is non-negotiable. No patterns, shadows, blurred backgrounds, or colors. The reasoning is simple: passport officials need a clean, distraction-free view of your face for comparison to future documents. Many people use a white bedsheet, poster board, or even paint a small section of wall. As long as it is bright and uniform, it works.

Expression and Pose

Your face should take up about 50 percent of the frame. Look straight ahead at the camera with a neutral expression. Most countries specifically prohibit smiling. Your eyes must be open and clearly visible. No squinting. Tilt your head slightly if it feels more natural, but typically head position should be square to the camera. Ideally, your ears should be visible on both sides.

Glasses and Headgear

Most countries have moved away from allowing regular glasses in passport photos unless you wear them permanently for a medical reason. Even then, you may need to provide documentation or take a separate photo without glasses. Some nations still permit reading glasses if they are prescribed, but the lens must not create glare and your eyes must be clearly visible. Religious headgear is typically permitted as long as it does not obscure the facial features needed for identification.

Official Size Specifications by Country

Size is often where people run into trouble. Submitting a photo that does not match your country's specifications is a guaranteed rejection. Below are the official dimensions for four major countries. When you take your photo with a smartphone, you will have a digital file that is typically much larger than the final photo size. Using free or low-cost photo cropping apps designed for passport photos makes hitting these dimensions straightforward. Apps exist for iOS and Android that let you input your country and automatically crop to spec.

Lighting and Image Quality

Poor lighting is one of the most common reasons for rejection. Shadows across the face, uneven illumination, or blown-out areas around the face disqualify the photo.

Best Lighting Practices

Natural light from a window is your best resource. Position yourself perpendicular to the window so light falls evenly across your face. You want to avoid harsh shadows under the eyes or along one side of your face. Overcast days are ideal because the clouds diffuse the light evenly. Avoid direct sunlight at midday, which can create unflattering shadows and cause you to squint.

What this means in practice

You take a photo with a north-facing window on a cloudy afternoon and notice your face has a soft shadow under your left eye. Reposition so the light hits you evenly from both sides, or wait for a sunnier day. Submit the wrong version and your application is rejected for "insufficient facial definition"—a two-week delay.

Never use your phone's camera flash. Flash tends to create harsh shadows, washes out skin tone, and often causes red-eye. If your room is dark, move closer to the window or choose a different time of day. Better to retake the photo in good light than to submit a flash-lit photo that gets rejected.

Use a tripod or prop your phone against a stable object so you have both hands free to position your face correctly. This also ensures the camera is level and focused on your face. If you must hold the phone, have someone else take the photo to avoid camera shake and uneven framing.

Resolution and Focus

Modern smartphones have excellent cameras. A modern phone camera typically shoots at 12 megapixels or higher, which is more than sufficient. Ensure your phone is in focus mode before taking the shot. Most phones will focus on your face if you tap on your face on the screen before shooting. The final image should be sharp, not blurry.

Common Rejection Reasons and How to Avoid Them

Understanding why photos are rejected helps you avoid the same mistakes. Below are the most frequent issues officials cite when they reject passport photos.

  • Shadows on the face: Caused by side lighting or harsh light sources. Solution: Use window light positioned perpendicular to your face. Check that your ears and cheekbones are evenly lit.
  • Incorrect photo dimensions: Photo does not match your country's specifications (requirements vary by country). Solution: Use a passport photo cropping app and double-check dimensions before printing or uploading. Compare against your authority's official spec, not another country's.
  • Smiling or unusual expression: Most countries require neutral expression. Solution: Practice a calm, straight-ahead look in a mirror before you take the shot. Look serious, not stern.
  • Glasses with glare: Glare on lenses obscures eyes. Solution: Remove glasses or adjust angle to eliminate reflection. Many countries now prohibit glasses entirely unless medically necessary — check your authority's current rules.
  • Blurry or out-of-focus image: Motion or focus issues during capture. Solution: Use a tripod or stable object to prop your phone, tap the screen to focus on your face, and avoid any movement during the shot.
  • Wrong background: Coloured background, pattern, or uneven white background. Solution: Use plain white or off-white poster board or bedsheet with no visible texture or shadows.
  • Excessive head room or cropping: Face too small or positioned incorrectly in frame. Solution: Ensure face takes up approximately 50 percent of the frame per your country's specifications.

From Smartphone to Official Photo: The Process

Once you have taken a good smartphone photo, the next steps are cropping to spec and either printing or uploading digitally, depending on your country's process.

Cropping Tools

Free and low-cost apps are available for iOS and Android that are specifically designed to crop photos to passport dimensions. Examples include Passport Photo Online, ID Photo Studio, and similar tools available in the App Store or Google Play. These apps typically let you select your country, and they automatically crop and check that your face dimensions are correct. Some apps also provide feedback if your lighting or background does not meet standards.

Printing vs. Digital Submission

Some countries accept digital uploads of the biometric passport photo submitted online. Others require printed physical photos. Check your passport authority's current submission process before you take your photo. If printing, use a professional photo printer or take your digital file to a pharmacy or print shop. Home inkjet printers typically produce acceptable results, but colour accuracy and paper quality matter. Photo paper is inexpensive and ensures better colour fidelity than standard printer paper.

Keep a digital copy of your approved biometric passport photo in a secure place. You may need to reference the exact dimensions or specifications during future renewals. Preparation Checklist

Before you take your photo, ensure you have the following in place:

  • Plain white or off-white background material ready and positioned
  • Good natural light source (preferably a window)
  • Smartphone with a functioning camera
  • Tripod or stable object to prop your phone
  • Knowledge of your country's exact photo specifications
  • A cropping app downloaded and ready to use
  • Plan for printing or digital submission based on your country's requirements

Spending a few minutes to prepare properly increases the likelihood that your photo will be accepted on the first attempt, saving you time and frustration during an already lengthy passport application process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take a passport photo on my smartphone +

Yes, you can take a passport photo using a smartphone. Position yourself in front of a plain white or off-white background with good natural light, and ensure the photo meets your country's size and quality requirements. Many people then use a cropping app to resize the digital photo to official dimensions and either print it or upload it digitally depending on their country's submission process.

What size should a passport photo be +

Size requirements vary significantly by country. The US requires 2x2 inches (51x51mm) — see travel.state.gov. The UK and Australia require 35x45mm — see gov.uk and passports.gov.au. Canada requires 50x70mm with the face occupying 31-36mm — see canada.ca. Specifications must be exact. Always check your passport authority before submitting.

Why was my passport photo rejected +

Common rejection reasons include shadows on the face, incorrect size, smiling or unusual expressions, wearing glasses, blurry images, or improper background. Ensure your photo meets all official requirements before submission. Review the specific feedback from your passport authority and retake the photo addressing each issue mentioned.

Do I need special lighting for a passport photo +

Natural light from a window is ideal for passport photos. Avoid harsh shadows on your face and do not use harsh camera flash. Position yourself facing the light source directly or perpendicular to a window so the light falls evenly across your face. Overcast days provide ideal diffused light without harsh shadows.

Can I wear glasses in a passport photo +

Most countries now prohibit glasses in passport photos unless medically necessary. Even then, you may need to provide documentation. Check your country's current requirements before taking your photo, as rules have evolved in recent years. When in doubt, take both a photo with and without glasses to have options.
